\begin{abstract}
\noindent
\code{ceiloclass} classifies atmospheric targets --- cloud droplets, supercooled
liquid, ice, drizzle/rain and aerosol --- from a \emph{single} ceilometer or
lidar (attenuated backscatter, plus depolarization on the Vaisala CL61) together
with a Cloudnet numerical-weather-model temperature field. It carries \emph{no}
cloud radar, and is a port of the radar-free parts of CloudnetPy's categorize and
target-classification code. The pipeline is one-directional:
\textbf{read $\rightarrow$ model $\rightarrow$ detect $\rightarrow$ classify
$\rightarrow$ plot}. This paper documents each step, the physical reasoning
behind it, and --- importantly --- the places where the implementation
\emph{deliberately diverges} from CloudnetPy to compensate for the missing radar.
The three principal divergences are a data-adaptive backscatter threshold, a
top-most freezing-level crossing, and (on the CL61) the use of depolarization to
anchor the ice/rain boundary to observed ice rather than to the raw model
\Tzero{} level.
\end{abstract}

%======================================================================
\section{Introduction}
%======================================================================

A ceilometer measures range-corrected attenuated backscatter $\beta(t, z)$ as a
function of time $t$ and range $z$. Unlike a cloud radar it cannot, on its own,
separate hydrometeor phase: a bright backscatter return may be a liquid droplet
cloud, falling ice, or dense aerosol. CloudnetPy resolves this ambiguity by
combining lidar with radar reflectivity, Doppler velocity and
linear depolarization. \code{ceiloclass} reconstructs as much of that
classification as possible \emph{without} the radar, leaning instead on:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item the \textbf{model temperature field}, which fixes where the air is warm
        enough for liquid and cold enough for ice;
  \item the \textbf{shape of the backscatter profile}, which distinguishes a
        sharp liquid-droplet peak from a diffuse aerosol or ice return;
  \item \textbf{linear depolarization} on the CL61, which directly separates
        (spherical, weakly depolarizing) liquid from (non-spherical, strongly
        depolarizing) ice.
\end{enumerate}

The output is a per-pixel \target{Target} category on the ceilometer
$(\text{time}\times\text{range})$ grid. Reading and harmonizing raw instrument
data is delegated to \code{ceilopyter}, which yields a \code{Ceilo} object
exposing \code{time}, \code{range}, \code{beta} (screened backscatter, a masked
array whose mask means ``no signal'') and, when available, \code{depol}. The same
pipeline runs unchanged on anything \code{ceilopyter} reads into a \code{Ceilo}:
besides ceilometers, the harmonized PollyXT, DIAL (e.g.\ the Vaisala DA10) and
doppler-lidar products, all carrying ceilometer-scale backscatter --- only the
CL61 adds depolarization, so every other instrument takes the no-depol path.

\paragraph{Notation.}
Throughout, $\beta$ is the screened backscatter (\betau), $z$ the range above the
instrument (m), $t$ time, $T_w$ the wet-bulb temperature (K), and
$\Tzero = 273.16\,\mathrm{K}$ the freezing threshold (the triple point of water)
used everywhere as the liquid/ice temperature boundary. A boolean field over the
grid is called a \emph{mask}; ``signal'' denotes the unmasked pixels
$\neg\,\mathrm{mask}(\beta)$.

%======================================================================
\section{Model temperature processing}
\label{sec:model}
%======================================================================

The model provides temperature $T$, pressure $p$ and specific humidity $q$ on a
coarse $(\text{time}\times\text{height})$ grid. Three things must happen before
it can be used: the vertical coordinate must be reconciled with the ceilometer's,
the fields must be interpolated onto the observation grid, and the wet-bulb
temperature must be derived.

\subsection{Grid alignment in absolute coordinates}

Model height is measured above the model grid cell's surface; ceilometer range is
measured above the instrument. In complex terrain the two reference surfaces can
differ by hundreds of metres (e.g.\ $\sim$270\,m at Granada). Given the site
altitude $a$ (m a.s.l.) and the model's own surface altitude $s_i$ at time step
$i$ --- taken from \code{sfc\_height}, or from \code{sfc\_geopotential}$/g$ with
$g = 9.80665\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$ --- the per-step correction is
%
\begin{equation}
  c_i = s_i - a ,
\end{equation}
%
and each model profile is queried at $z - c_i$ rather than at $z$, placing both
grids in absolute (above-sea-level) coordinates. When either altitude is unknown,
$c_i = 0$ and both grids are treated as height-above-ground.

\subsection{Interpolation, then wet-bulb (order matters)}

Each kept model profile is interpolated onto the observation range by linear
interpolation that \emph{extrapolates} beyond the model levels rather than
clamping (\code{interp\_extrap}, matching
\code{scipy}'s \code{fill\_value="extrapolate"}), so a site sitting below the
lowest model level still receives a sensible near-ground value. The result is
then interpolated along time (clamped at the temporal edges).

Wet-bulb temperature is computed \emph{from the already-interpolated} $T$, $p$,
$q$, via \code{atmoslib} (falling back to dry-bulb if unavailable). This ordering
matches CloudnetPy and matters because wet-bulb is nonlinear in its inputs:
interpolating a wet-bulb field built on the coarse model grid would not equal the
wet-bulb of the interpolated fields. The solve is restricted to gates with
$p > 1000\,\mathrm{Pa}$ to guard the extrapolated near-vacuum gates above the
model top.

A model time step with no finite temperature/height values (occasionally seen in
HARMONIE files) is dropped and bridged by the time interpolation, rather than
failing the whole classification.

\subsection{The extrapolated (quality) flag}

The returned \code{Model} carries a boolean \code{extrapolated} field, true where
$T_w$ was extrapolated \emph{above the model top} or \emph{outside the model's
temporal coverage}. Outside the model levels the value is not clamped but linearly
extrapolated along the edge slope (\code{interp\_extrap}). Extrapolation
\emph{below} the lowest model level is deliberately \emph{not} flagged --- in
complex terrain a site can sit below the model surface, and the extrapolation
there gives a sensible near-ground value. The flag is surfaced downstream as the
\code{quality} field of the classification, marking lower-confidence pixels.

\subsection{The freezing level \texorpdfstring{$\Tzero$}{T0}}
\label{sec:t0}

The single most important model-derived quantity is the altitude of the
$0\,\degc$ isotherm, computed per profile by \code{\_find\_t0\_alt}. Given two
adjacent gates $a, b$ that straddle \Tzero{}, the crossing altitude is the linear
interpolation
%
\begin{equation}
  z_{\Tzero} = z_a + (\Tzero - T_w[a])\,\frac{z_b - z_a}{T_w[b] - T_w[a]} .
  \label{eq:t0}
\end{equation}
%
\textbf{Which crossing is chosen is a deliberate divergence from CloudnetPy.}
CloudnetPy always takes the lowest \Tzero{} crossing. \code{ceiloclass} instead:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item with a \emph{warm surface} ($T_w[0] \ge \Tzero$, the usual case) takes the
        \emph{lowest} crossing (gates $a=j-1$, $b=j$ with $j$ the lowest cold
        gate), so the boundary tracks the melting level smoothly even through
        warm--cold--warm layering aloft;
  \item with a \emph{sub-freezing surface} --- a winter surface inversion --- takes
        the \emph{topmost} crossing (gates $a=j$, $b=j+1$ with $j$ the highest
        warm gate). The lowest crossing would otherwise collapse to the ground and
        mislabel the entire warm column above the inversion as ice.
\end{itemize}
Everything above $z_{\Tzero}$ is the \emph{freezing region}
(\code{find\_freezing\_region}): the candidate domain for ice and supercooled
liquid.

%======================================================================
\section{Detection primitives}
\label{sec:detect}
%======================================================================

Each function in \code{detection.py} is a pure boolean mask over the grid, ported
from CloudnetPy and restricted to the radar-free signal. They are composed by
\code{classify} (Section~\ref{sec:classify}).

\subsection{Liquid droplet layers}
\label{sec:liquid}

\code{find\_liquid} detects liquid layers from the \emph{shape} of the
backscatter profile: a liquid cloud base produces a sharp peak with a strong
gradient above it (the lidar is rapidly attenuated inside the droplets). The
algorithm is CloudnetPy's \code{droplet.find\_liquid} minus the
liquid-water-path and radar top-correction steps (no radiometer/radar):

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Find strict local maxima of $\beta$ along range (\code{local\_maxima},
        comparing $\pm 4$ neighbours) exceeding a peak amplitude
        $\beta_{\mathrm{peak}} = 10^{-6}\,\betau$.
  \item For each peak, walk down to a \emph{base} and up to a \emph{top} using the
        backscatter difference profile, searching at most $200\,\mathrm{m}$ below
        and $150\,\mathrm{m}$ above.
  \item Accept the peak as liquid (\code{\_is\_valid\_peak}) only if it has at
        least $3$ non-zero gates, a thickness $< 250\,\mathrm{m}$, a peak altitude
        $\ge 100\,\mathrm{m}$ above the lowest gate, and a top gradient
        \[
          \frac{\beta[\text{peak}] - \beta[\text{top}]}
               {z[\text{top}] - z[\text{peak}]} > 10^{-7} .
        \]
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Surface pass (extension beyond CloudnetPy).}
A strict local maximum can never fall within \code{order} gates of a profile
edge, so the lowest $2\times 4 = 8$ gates form a \emph{blind zone}. Fog and very
low stratus peaking there would be invisible to the main search and fall through
to aerosol. A second pass therefore takes the strongest gate in the blind zone,
anchors the base at the ground, walks the top upward as usual, and applies the
same validity test with the minimum-altitude criterion relaxed (liquid sitting on
the ground by definition).

\paragraph{Cloud-strength base (extension beyond CloudnetPy).}
The base search follows the backscatter difference profile down to its
$200\,\mathrm{m}$ window limit. On a hazy day the sub-cloud aerosol gradient is
gradual, so the base is dragged $\sim$200\,m down into haze --- and \code{peak\_amp}
($10^{-6}\,\betau$) sits far below the adaptive cloud threshold
$\beta_{\mathrm{strong}}$, so a weak bump whose \emph{whole} layer stays below
$\beta_{\mathrm{strong}}$ is even flagged as liquid. The base is therefore raised
to the layer's lowest cloud-strength gate ($\beta \ge \beta_{\mathrm{strong}}$),
and a peak that never reaches cloud strength is rejected outright. The bright cloud
core is kept while the sub-cloud haze collapses back to aerosol; the
\code{grow\_liquid}/\code{fill\_thin\_clouds} halo recovery (Section~\ref{sec:grow})
still runs afterwards.

\subsection{Cold elevated signal (falling ice)}
\label{sec:falling}

\code{find\_falling} is the lidar-only branch of CloudnetPy's falling-hydrometeor
detection: backscatter present in cold air is treated as ice. A pixel is
\emph{falling} when it carries signal, lies above $2000\,\mathrm{m}$, and is
colder than $\mathrm{COLD\_LIMIT} = \Tzero - 15 = -15\,\degc$. Above
$4000\,\mathrm{m}$ an \emph{isolated} single gate (fewer than two signal gates in
a $\pm 3$-gate window) is dropped, to avoid mislabelling thin aerosol as ice.

\subsection{Depolarization ice (CL61 only)}
\label{sec:depolice}

On the CL61, linear depolarization gives directly what CloudnetPy otherwise gets
from radar. \code{find\_depol\_ice} marks every signal pixel whose depolarization
exceeds $\mathrm{ICE\_DEPOL\_LIMIT} = 0.15$ as ice:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{ice\_like} = \neg\,\mathrm{mask}(\beta)\ \wedge\ (\delta > 0.15).
\end{equation}
%
Liquid droplets are spherical and barely depolarize ($\delta < 0.1$); ice
crystals are non-spherical and depolarize strongly ($\delta \sim 0.3$--$0.5$). The
threshold sits between the two populations. This both vetoes \code{find\_liquid}
false positives (ice virga read as a droplet layer) and recovers ice that
\code{find\_falling} misses below its altitude/temperature cutoffs.

\subsection{Growing and filling liquid layers}
\label{sec:grow}

\code{find\_liquid} marks only the sharp core of a layer; the weaker gates hugging
its base and top are still cloud but fall outside the gradient bounds. Two
operators absorb them:

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=1.2em, nosep]
  \item[\code{grow\_liquid}] dilates the droplet mask along range into adjacent
        gates that carry signal and are not \emph{blocked} (ice). Growth is
        \emph{asymmetric}: generous upward ($100\,\mathrm{m}$, to recover the
        attenuated true top), minimal downward ($10\,\mathrm{m}$, to avoid
        swallowing sub-cloud aerosol/drizzle). Distances are converted to gate
        counts for the instrument's resolution.
  \item[\code{fill\_thin\_clouds}] relabels a \emph{whole} contiguous signal run
        as liquid when it contains a droplet gate and is no thicker than
        $100\,\mathrm{m}$. Thicker runs (a cloud over a deep aerosol column) are
        left alone so genuine aerosol below the cloud is not absorbed.
\end{description}

\subsection{Supercooled limit}

\code{correct\_supercooled} removes droplet gates colder than
$\mathrm{SUPERCOOLED\_LIMIT} = \Tzero - 38 = -38\,\degc$, below which liquid water
cannot exist.

\section{The adaptive backscatter threshold}
\label{sec:threshold}
%======================================================================

The pivotal decision in radar-free classification is: \emph{how bright
must signal be to count as cloud/precipitation rather than aerosol?} A
fixed threshold fails because aerosol load varies enormously by site
and day (a dusty Granada afternoon vs.\ a clean Arctic
morning). \code{\_adaptive\_strong\_beta} therefore derives the
threshold $\beta_{\mathrm{strong}}$ from the data distribution itself.
This is the second major divergence from CloudnetPy.

The cloud--aerosol backscatter threshold, $\beta_{\mathrm{strong}}$,
is derived adaptively from the observed backscatter distribution
rather than prescribed as a fixed value. This accounts for the large
day-to-day and site-to-site variability in aerosol loading (e.g. a
dusty continental boundary layer versus a clean Arctic atmosphere).

All positive backscatter values in the ceilometer profile are
histogrammed in logarithmic space between the 1st and 99.9th
percentiles using 60 bins, and the histogram is lightly smoothed with
a three-bin moving average to suppress noise. The lowest well-defined
mode is taken as the aerosol mode. This is not necessarily the
histogram maximum, since on heavily overcast days the cloud mode may
contain more samples than the aerosol population. A lower-valued mode
is therefore preferred whenever it is separated from the dominant peak
by a genuine minimum in the histogram, preventing small fluctuations
on the shoulder of a broad peak from being interpreted as independent
aerosol modes.

The threshold is then placed according to the histogram morphology. If
a second mode exists at substantially larger backscatter (more than
twice the aerosol-mode value and exceeding
$3\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm{sr^{-1}\,m^{-1}}$), it is interpreted as
cloud or precipitation, and the threshold is placed at the minimum
between the aerosol and cloud modes. Otherwise, the distribution is
treated as aerosol only (possibly with multiple aerosol layers), and
the threshold is placed on the right-hand shoulder of the aerosol
mode, where the histogram has fallen to 5\% of its peak value. When
multiple aerosol modes are present, the shoulder is referenced to the
highest aerosol mode so that elevated aerosol layers (e.g. lofted
Saharan dust) remain classified as aerosol rather than being separated
as cloud.

Finally, the threshold is bounded by both a relative limit (25 times
the aerosol-mode backscatter) and an absolute limit of
$10^{-5}\,\mathrm{sr^{-1}\,m^{-1}}$. The relative bound acts only as a
conservative fallback if neither a distinct valley nor shoulder can be
identified, whereas the absolute bound prevents unrealistically large
thresholds in cases where aerosol and cloud are not separable, such as
persistent polar low-cloud conditions. If fewer than 1000 valid
backscatter samples are available, the algorithm returns the default
threshold of $3\times10^{-6}\,\mathrm{sr^{-1}\,m^{-1}}$.

Figure~\ref{fig:adaptive_beta} illustrates four representative
histogram morphologies encountered by the adaptive threshold
algorithm, demonstrating how the threshold is placed for bimodal cloud
cases, multilayer aerosol, clean conditions, and polar-winter
low-cloud continua.

\section{Classification assembly}
\label{sec:classify}
%======================================================================

\code{classify(ceilo, model)} composes the primitives into the final category
grid. The skeleton, ignoring depolarization for a moment, is:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Compute the freezing region (Section~\ref{sec:t0}) and the liquid layers
        (Section~\ref{sec:liquid}); grow and fill them
        (Section~\ref{sec:grow}); apply the supercooled limit.
  \item Pick $\beta_{\mathrm{strong}}$ (Section~\ref{sec:threshold}).
  \item Mark \emph{bright} signal: $\neg\,\mathrm{mask}(\beta) \wedge
        (\beta > \beta_{\mathrm{strong}})$. \emph{Strong} signal is bright and
        non-droplet.
  \item \textbf{Ice} $=$ strong signal in the freezing region.
        \textbf{Drizzle/rain} $=$ strong signal in the warm region (below
        \Tzero), gated on a source above it (Section~\ref{sec:source}).
        \textbf{Aerosol} $=$ any remaining signal.
  \item Assemble category codes (Section~\ref{sec:assemble}) and despeckle.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Depolarization refinements (CL61 only)}

When depolarization is available, \code{ice\_like} (Section~\ref{sec:depolice})
sharpens three things:

\paragraph{(a) Multiple-scattering tail.}
\emph{Inside} a liquid layer, depolarization climbs from multiple scattering and
would falsely trip the ice veto, carving out the densest part of a real droplet
cloud. Genuine liquid layers are identified (they contain a low-depol
single-scattering part that pure ice lacks) and shielded --- the whole layer plus
a short tail of $\mathrm{MS\_TAIL} = 80\,\mathrm{m}$ above it (the
$\sim$90th-percentile observed tail) --- from the veto. With depolarization
available, \code{ice\_like} also \emph{replaces} \code{find\_falling}'s
$-15\,\degc / 2000\,\mathrm{m}$ heuristic as the barrier to liquid growth, so a
genuine high-altitude supercooled cloud top (e.g.\ at Troll) is not clipped.

\paragraph{(b) Extending the freezing region down to observed ice.}
A model \Tzero{} level biased \emph{high} leaves depol-confirmed ice on its warm
side (falling ice not yet melted). \code{\_extend\_cold\_to\_ice} floods the
freezing region \emph{downward} through contiguous \emph{solid-ice} gates --- but
no more than $\mathrm{max\_depth} = 1500\,\mathrm{m}$ below the original boundary,
so lofted dust touching cloud cannot drag the whole column sub-freezing. Two
conditions gate the flood:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=(\roman*)]
  \item \emph{Cloud strength} ($\beta > \beta_{\mathrm{strong}}$): a daytime
        boundary layer can be full of weakly-backscattering yet strongly-
        depolarizing aerosol (dust, pollen) that would otherwise reach the ground.
  \item \emph{Solid-ice depolarization}
        ($\delta > \mathrm{ICE\_CORE\_DEPOL\_LIMIT} = 0.30$, well above the $0.15$
        ice/liquid limit of Section~\ref{sec:depolice}). Across the melting level
        depolarization drops from solid ice ($\sim$0.4--0.5) to the $\sim$0.2 of
        the wet/melting rain shaft below it, so the solid-ice base sits at that
        drop. Requiring solid-ice depol stops the flood \emph{at the phase change}
        rather than running on through the still-depolarizing rain below \Tzero{},
        which would otherwise drag the ice region far into warm air.
\end{enumerate}
The new ice base is then smoothed against noisy single-profile pillars: the
per-profile base height is replaced by its rolling median over $\pm 10$ profiles
and the extension clipped to it. Keying on the base being an outlier (rather than
on a profile count) makes it insensitive to time averaging and to clustered
pillars. Figure~\ref{fig:extendice} shows a CL61 case where this applies.

\centering

\caption{\code{\_extend\_cold\_to\_ice} on a CL61 case (panels: screened

\paragraph{(c) The melt band (extending rain up to observed ice).}
The symmetric counterpart: when a depol-ice base sits \emph{above} the model
\Tzero, the real melting level is higher than the model's, and the cold (per the
model), cloud-strength, low-depol band between them is ice \emph{melting into
rain}, not ice. \code{\_melt\_band\_below\_ice} marks the band as the
cloud-strength signal reachable \emph{both} downward from the depol ice base
\emph{and} upward from the warm region --- a continuous column linking the ice
down to the rain across the melting level. Requiring both ends excludes low-depol
patches buried inside an ice cloud and supercooled layers with no ice above them.
Because the melting layer itself depolarizes (wet, irregular particles), a
\emph{thin} ($\le 150\,\mathrm{m}$) high-depol enhancement at the melt level is
bridged rather than allowed to block, while a thick coherent ice cloud still
blocks. The band is then dropped from the freezing region, so it classifies as
drizzle/rain. Figure~\ref{fig:meltband} shows this on a stratiform-rain day.

Note the asymmetry with~(b): the downward extension keys on the solid-ice limit
($0.30$), but the melt band keys on the \emph{low} ice/liquid limit ($0.15$).
Above a biased-\emph{low} \Tzero{} the air is genuinely warm and the melted drops
are near-spherical, so the rain there has $\delta < 0.15$; a $0.15$--$0.30$
depolarization on the \emph{cold} side of \Tzero{} is still cold ice and must
\emph{not} be stripped to rain, which a solid-ice threshold here would do.

\subsection{The drizzle source gate}
\label{sec:source}

Drizzle and rain fall \emph{from} a cloud: precipitation and its parent cloud form
one contiguous signal column. \code{\_source\_connected} keeps a warm bright gate
as drizzle/rain only when a cloud gate (liquid layer or ice) lies \emph{above} it
and every gate between carries signal. This rejects two false sources:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item a bright layer separated from anything above by clear air (e.g.\ a
        near-surface aerosol blob far below an unrelated cirrus --- the path is
        broken);
  \item a cloud \emph{below} the layer (e.g.\ haze sitting above a shallow surface
        fog --- the cloud is not above it). This is what keeps the cloud-free
        bright marine haze at Mindelo classified as aerosol, not drizzle.
\end{itemize}
The cloud mask may first be dilated by $\pm\,$\code{drizzle\_source\_window}
profiles in time, so a single-profile gap at a ragged cloud edge does not drop a
genuine drizzle shaft beside it. The default window is $0$ (source required in the
same profile); a negative value disables the gate entirely.

\paragraph{Bridging the melting-layer notch.}
Requiring \emph{strictly} continuous signal has a failure mode at the melting
level. A melting layer produces a backscatter notch that frequently falls below
the screening threshold, leaving a thin masked band between the rain below and the
ice above --- and a single masked gate breaks the path, so the whole drizzle
column beneath it loses its source and falls through to aerosol. The gate
therefore bridges a clear-air run no longer than
$\mathrm{DRIZZLE\_SOURCE\_MAX\_GAP} = 100\,\mathrm{m}$, bounded by signal on both
sides, before tracing the path. This reconnects the drizzle across the screened
melt notch while still rejecting the Mindelo case: a kilometre-scale clear gap has
an unbridged core far longer than $100\,\mathrm{m}$, so its path stays broken. The
distance is the discriminator the bare continuity test lacked --- a melt notch is
tens of metres, an unrelated cirrus sits kilometres up. On a stratiform-rain CL61
day at Lindenberg this recovers $\sim$6\,000 gates from aerosol to drizzle, of
which $98\%$ are confirmed precipitation or melting by the co-located radar
Cloudnet product (the same gates it had mislabelled aerosol).

\subsection{Category precedence and despeckling}
\label{sec:assemble}

\code{\_assemble} writes the category codes with later rules overwriting earlier
ones, encoding the precedence ``liquid layers sit on top'':
%
\begin{center}
\small
aerosol $\rightarrow$ ice $\rightarrow$ drizzle/rain $\rightarrow$
droplet (warm) $\rightarrow$ supercooled (droplet $\wedge$ cold).
\end{center}
%
Finally \code{\_despeckle} clears any classified pixel with fewer than
\code{speckle\_min} (default $3$) classified pixels in its $3\times3$
neighbourhood, removing isolated specks left by screening noise. It is computed
efficiently with a 2-D summed-area (integral-image) table.

%======================================================================
\section{Limitations}
%======================================================================

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Without radar, phase for non-CL61 instruments rests entirely on the model
        temperature and backscatter shape; the freezing region is a model
        quantity and inherits its biases (mitigated, on the CL61, by the
        depol-anchored boundary).
  \item The lidar is rapidly attenuated inside thick liquid; nothing can be said
        about cloud above an optically thick base.
  \item The adaptive threshold assumes a recognizable aerosol mode; in a
        featureless distribution it falls back to fixed defaults and caps.
  \item There is no melting-layer or insect class, and no liquid-water-path
        constraint (no radiometer).
\end{itemize}
